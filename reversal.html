colour, aspect ratio 4:3 (adjusted for mobile)



The transition to digital and post-cinematic media has precipitated a decoupling of moving images from human subjectivity and perspective. Though we still view and process images related to the cinematic era, this media is now mediated in subtle ways that erode the perceived distance and relationship between viewers as phenomenological subjects and onscreen objects as perceptual objects. The once tangible sense of perspective distance, the space between subjective viewer and viewed object, has been distorted. In short, digitisation has quietly corrupted the visual language of traditional cinema that tethered images to human vantage points. Thus, while retaining a semblance of cinema's imagery, the digitised moving image diverges from human situatedness and veers into non-human territories yet-to-be-accounted-for. Our visual embodiments no longer align with the visual data streaming before our eyes. Moving images float free of embodied people in physical spaces, their meaning adrift. As 'Reversal' perpetually cycles both forward and backward through the entire 360 degree color spectrum, the artwork immerses viewers in a radically non-representational digitality severed from human subjectivity. In so doing, the work's dissociative images underline the loss of grounded perspective and agency in our post-cinematic age, perhaps signaling greater public vulnerability to external control. Though ‘Reversal’ echoes cinema’s visual flow, its endless colour wheel immerses viewers in a hypnotic yet disembodied space. In severing ties to reference and representation, the artwork traps attention in an economy dictated by the algorithmic loop. Unable to orient in the revolving spectra, subjects lose footing in phenomenological reality.  This induces a loss of agency in ascribing significance and crafting meaning from the cascading colours. The work undermines the viewer's authority over their senses and capacity for critical thinking. Consequently, detached from internal anchors of subjectivity, the viewer becomes more vulnerable to projected external narratives and ideologies that can subliminally hunt via the logic of the senses.  This reveals how digital media threatens to deprive society of sovereignty over its reality, leaving the post-cinematic public susceptible to ideological programming and control through hypnotically discorrelated images. ‘Reversal’ reflects the political economic context that spawned such disembodied media. The privatisation of perception dovetails with the neoliberal logic of recent decades. As public goods and spaces disappeared into private hands, so too has the territorialisation of consciousness and the sensory commons accelerated. The outsourcing of subjective orientation to algorithmic activities parallels the depletion of communal ties and institutional palisades against all-encompassing markets. Like climate change and the financialisation of healthcare, the post-cinematic unmooring of images from human perspective grew from the assumptions underscoring late capitalism.  Works like ‘Reversal’ emerge as artistic warnings from the future portending what awaits once collective agency fully dissolves into market forces and data flows. The piece reveals a experiential realm where citizens lack the phenomenological tools necessary to challenge hypercommercialised political spaces or to even recognise rights once held sacred.

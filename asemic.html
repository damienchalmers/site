This system can be used for subversive purposes.Because asemic writing doesn't adhere to any established language system, it can be used to convey messages that are hidden from those who don't understand the code. This can be seen as a political act, as it allows for communication outside of the dominant power structures.

Asemic writing can challenge the status quo of language and communication.By creating a new form of language, asemic writing can be seen as a challenge to the established norms and hierarchies of communication. This can be seen as a political act, as it disrupts the way that power is exercised through language.

The interpretation of asemic writing can be subjective and open to multiple interpretations.This can be seen as a political act, as it challenges the idea of a single, objective truth and instead allows for the possibility of multiple perspectives.

Asemic writing, through its form and structure, can intimate a type of document and thus hint at meaning. This illegible style, frequently calligraphic, either parasitises a reader's knowledge of writing systems for comprehension or is understood through (anti)aesthetic (non)sense. Genuine asemic works occur when the creator cannot decipher their own asemic writing. It is a creative production that paradoxically retreats the more that it proliferates. Moderately asemic writing includes natural writing systems legible to some but not all (e.g., codes, stylised scripts). Most asemic writing falls between these extremes.
Influences include indecipherable, invented, or primal scripts like wall paintings, scribbles, primitive divination, witch marks, broken runes, or children's attempts. Rather than imitating preliterate expression, asemic writing embodies a global postliterate style drawing inspiration broadly. Other influences are exotic languages in sci-fi, artistic argots, symbols, unsolved scripts, lingua ignota, and street art. Uses include catalysing mental and creative ideas, wordless communication, contemplation, hoax, overcoming writer's block, privacy, secrecy, subterfuge, and the liberatory potentialities of general authorial uncertainty.


The work is part of the larger exosemantic project We Learned Another Language to Not Tell You This.
